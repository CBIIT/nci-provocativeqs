Submitted by: Mohandas Mallath
August 14, 2014

Is the consumption of large volumes (above physiological limits) of acidified carbonated soft drinks repeatedly from early life responsible for high prevalence rates of gastro-esophageal reflux disease, and Barrett’s esophagus and the increasing incidence of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus (ACE) in North America and all countries with high per capita carbonated soft drink consumption? Slowly repeated esophageal acidification like passive smoking.

Submitted By Mohandas Mallath

Adenocarcinoma of Esophagus in the United States (US) has raised six fold from 1975 to 2001. The rise of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus has been significant among younger patients aged 45 to 65 years with a cohort effect characterized by a 40% increase in incidence for each five-year increase in date of birth. Mathematical modeling suggests that the temporal trends of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus are dominated by dramatically increasing age effect. By 2006, a rise in the incidence of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus was reported from almost all the countries that belonged to the North Atlantic Treaty Organization including Belgium, Canada, Denmark, France, Iceland, Italy, Netherlands, Norway, Portugal, UK, and US. Contrasting was the absence of rising adenocarcinoma of the esophagus in East European countries including Czech Republic, Poland, Russia, Romania, Croatia, Estonia, and Slovak Republic that once belonged to the Warsaw Treaty Organization. The incidence rates of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus were higher in West German cancer registries in comparison to East German cancer registries. Among the Asia Pacific countries, adenocarcinoma of the esophagus has risen markedly in Australia and New Zealand and in several Middle Eastern counties including Jordan, Israel, Cyprus, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait. In contrast squamous carcinoma of the esophagus continues to constitute over 80% of esophageal cancers in Japan, Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, China, India, Pakistan, and Iran. Esophageal cancer is very common in sub-Saharan Africa and is almost entirely of the squamous carcinoma of the esophagus. Latin American countries reveal an increase in the proportion of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus in some countries, most prominent being in Mexico. In summary adenocarcinoma of the esophagus has started to increase in all countries with high per capita carbonated soft drinks, while squamous carcinoma of the esophagus has remained the predominant esophageal cancer in all countries with low per capita carbonated soft drink consumption. Temporally, the CSD drinking preceded the rise of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus by 20 years in a linear way. Gastro-esophageal reflux disease (GERD) is the most important risk factor for adenocarcinoma of the esophagus. The American Gastroenterology Association in their report on the burden of gastrointestinal diseases in US had estimated the total expenditure on GERD and Barrett’s Esophagus to be 10.17 billion in 1998 and 10.46 billion in 2000, which exceeds the total cost of managing all the colorectal, pancreatic and liver cancers. The substantial direct and indirect costs for managing GORD highlight the need of preventing this disease. Transient Lower Esophageal Sphincter Relaxations (TLOSR) is the main patho-physiological mechanism for GERD in patients with non-erosive disease, mild Esophagitis and severe reflux disease with or without hiatus hernias. The most important trigger for TLOSR is through gastric distension caused by meals, air or balloon. In children, the intra-gastric volume and the osmolality of the gastric contents are important determinants of reflux. The portion sizes of foods and beverages consumed by Americans at home, in restaurants and from take-away places have increased substantially in last few decades. The biggest increase was for carbonated soft drinks (CSD). By 2005 the CSD was the most popular beverage accounting for 28.3% of total fluid intake of the Americans. The CSD are highly acidic and hyperosmolar liquids which distends the stomach with liquid and gas. The CSD drinks possess several properties that can trigger TLOSR and cause reflux in children and adults. Volunteer study had confirmed the occurrence of TLOSR and acidification of the Esophagus after modest amounts of CSD. CSD drinking was found to trigger diurnal and nocturnal heartburn in three population surveys. It is therefore hypothesized that drinking of large volumes of CSD from childhood exposes the esophageal mucosa to low pH and initiate GERD, and some these may progress to adenocarcinoma of the esophagus over several decades. Two decades of research has not identified any cause for the continuous rise of this lethal cancer and its unique epidemiological features. This rise is not explained by the time trends of tobacco use or obesity. Smoking and obesity are more prevalent in the African Americans population who have low incidence of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus. The MONICA project of the World Health Organization shows that the obesity and smoking rates are more prevalent among East European populations compared to West Europeans. Based on regression analysis of a large sample of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus cases from several countries, researchers from International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) have suggested that the increase in the incidence of adenocarcinoma of the esophagus is real and might be explained by the introduction of a “new” environmental risk factor. Could the carbonated soft drink be the new environmental risk factor? Ecological studies do not offer sufficient proof of causality. Properly designed studies are urgently needed to put an end to the GERD and adenocarcinoma of the esophagus epidemics in soda drinking nations.
