Question ID: WS-87
Submitted by: John Groopman
April 11, 2011

What causes cancer? It is axiomatic that genomic instability and mutations are among the core processes in cancer cell development, but the specific agents causing these damages, particularly those of environmental origin,are still poorly understood. The statistical methods employed in epidemiology have probably led to the identification of the small set of highly mutagenic/carcinogenic compounds where human exposures have been high and/or the resultant tumors have been rare e.g. angiosarcoma and vinyl chloride. Thus, it is unlikely that we will discover many more single agents with the requisite potency and exposures and a significant discovery initiative is needed to identify multiplex interactions among these environmental agents that in turn accelerate the cancer process. In particular, special attention needs to be paid to the chemical-biological agent interactions that underlie many of the cancer cases around the world.

Submitted By Michael Lerman

Mutations in cancer genes cause and drive cancer growth and development. Cancer genes fall into two main categories: cancer-causing genes, CCG, that drive malignant transformation and maintain tumor growth, and CAN/progressor genes that drive and orchestrate local invasion and further spread of metastatic cells. CCG show high mutation rates (~100%) while the CAN genes show low mutation rates (5-10%); every cancer type/specimen is characterized by a specific assortment of CAN genes The number of genes involved in causation and cancer spread may be estimated from death frequencies as function of age (6-8 genetic steps to death from cancer) to be in the range of 3-4 assuming both alleles affected; allowing haploinsufficiency for some of these genes the number may be higher but not to exceed 4-6. This small number is therefore responsible for malignant transformation, initial local growth and finally cancer spread by invasion and metastasis culminating in the death of the patients. Here is an estimate of the number of CCG and CAN/driver genes. For one tumor type (e.g., ccRCC): 6-8 hits, 3-4 genes, 2-3 CAN genes with mutation frequencies 10% yields 20-30 genes per tumor type (allowing haploinsufficiency the number will increase to 40-60 genes respectively). For mutation frequencies of 5% these numbers will double. Finally, for 200 human tumor types (recognized by dedicated pathlogists) the number of CCG should be <200 and the number of CAN/driver genes should be in the range of 6,000 - 12, 000. These are mutated “driver” genes. Recently, cancer genome and exom sequencing have demonstrated two things: (1) the damage (mutations) are in too many genes and, therefore, (2) the mutation frequencies are as low as 1-5%.
